What it does

The statistics done right — then made publishable

CellMorphR is built around one conviction: with hierarchical microscopy data, the analysis you reach for by default is usually the wrong one. It fixes that first, then hands you figures a reviewer will trust.

🧬

Anti-pseudoreplication by default

Hundreds of cells per dish are not hundreds of independent data points. CellMorphR collapses each biological replicate to a single summary before any test runs, so your true N is the number of dishes — not the number of cells. This is the whole point of the tool.

📊

Two-way ANOVA on replicate summaries

A factorial ANOVA over Condition × Time on per-replicate medians. The Condition × Time interaction is the key test — it tells you whether the infection effect grows over time. Partial η² is reported for every term, matching Prism, SPSS, and JMP.

🔀

Linear mixed model alternative

For more power, fit Measurement ~ Condition * Time + (1|Rep) over all cell-level data. A random intercept absorbs the within-dish correlation; Satterthwaite p-values come from lmerTest. Replicate IDs are made unique across conditions automatically to avoid a silent modelling error.

📈

Replicate-level trajectories

The most important figure: each biological replicate's summary plotted over time, per condition, with loess trends and an SEM ribbon computed at the correct level. Diverging trajectories are the visual signature of the interaction you're reporting.

🎻

Seven distribution styles, SuperPlots included

Violin, strip, sina, box, ridge, ECDF, and a replicate-only dot plot (Lord et al., 2020) that shows just the N=3 the ANOVA actually used. Every style overlays per-replicate medians so statistical power is shown honestly, not hidden by a smooth curve.

🩺

Welch pairwise tests, Holm-corrected

Where the interaction is significant, pairwise Welch's t-tests on the replicate medians pinpoint when conditions diverge. Holm–Bonferroni controls the family-wise error rate, and the caption names the test, the N, the correction, and the number of comparisons.

📐

Effect sizes independent of p-values

Absolute difference, percent change from control, and replicate-level Cohen's d — plotted over time. With N=3, a real 30% enlargement can miss p<0.05; effect sizes say how big the effect is regardless. "Infected cells are 125% larger at 120 min" communicates biology directly.

🔬

Assumption diagnostics, run for you

Shapiro–Wilk on residuals and Levene's test with median centering (the same Levene variant Prism uses) run automatically and print into the Statistics tab. If an assumption is badly violated, you'll know before you submit.

🎨

Okabe-Ito colourblind-safe palettes

The default palette is now Okabe-Ito (viridis optional), scaling to any number of conditions — so your figures are legible to readers with colour-vision deficiency, not just to you. Selectable in the app and via --palette in the CLI.

🏷️

Unit-aware axis titles

Set a unit once and axis titles render as Area (µm²), Median Area (µm²), and so on across every tab and every panel. Leave it blank and nothing changes. Small thing; the kind of small thing a copy-editor otherwise sends back.

🔢

Real, computed replicate-N in captions

Captions used to hard-code "N=3". Now the actual replicate count per group is computed and printed — N = k, or N = lo–hi when the design is unbalanced — so the figure never claims a sample size the data doesn't have.

🖇️

Composite publication figures

Assemble panels A (distributions), B (trajectories), and C (effect sizes) into a single labelled, multi-panel figure with patchwork. Panel letters follow journal conventions; a global title and subtitle sit above the row.

📤

Five export formats, journal-ready

PDF and SVG and EPS vectors, plus PNG and LZW-compressed TIFF raster — at configurable DPI and dimensions on every tab. Defaults (300 DPI, PDF, sans-serif, 11 pt) already meet Nature / Science / Cell submission specs; drop to 8–9 pt for single-column.

🧪

ECDFs — assumption-free comparison

One panel per time point, one step per cell — no binning, no bandwidth. A rightward shift of the infected curve means larger cells; a tail-only divergence reveals a filamenting subpopulation the summary statistics would hide.

🔁

R and Python give identical numbers

Both implementations run the same ANOVA, the same Welch tests, and the same Holm correction, and agree to at least three decimal places on the same data. Explore interactively in Shiny; batch-process the same analysis in a pipeline with the CLI. The choice of tool never changes the result.

🗂️

Handles technical replicates correctly

Three images from one dish are technical replicates, not independent samples. Give them all the same Replicate value and CellMorphR pools them into one summary. Unequal cell counts across replicates are handled — each replicate contributes exactly one data point.

🧾

Machine-readable stats output

Save the full ANOVA and pairwise results as text, plus a pairwise_*.csv for downstream use. Demo data with a known ground-truth effect ships in both tools so you can validate your pipeline before you trust it on real experiments.

Why this exists

The story behind CellMorphR

In this post

The predicament The actual problem What I built On building tools Built with Claude

Measuring a cell is the part everyone thinks is the hard part. You segment your phase-contrast images, you pull out areas and perimeters and Feret diameters, and you end up with a beautiful spreadsheet: hundreds of cells per dish, several time points, a couple of conditions, three biological replicates. Thousands of rows. It feels like a mountain of data, and in a sense it is.

Then you have to do statistics to it, and that is where the quiet trouble starts.

The temptation is almost irresistible. You have four thousand eight hundred cells sitting right there. Every one of them is a real measurement of a real cell. So you drop the whole column into a t-test or an ANOVA, and the software cheerfully reports p < 0.0001, and the difference between your conditions looks bulletproof. It is the most natural thing in the world to do, and it is wrong.

It is dangerously easy to treat hundreds of cells from three dishes as hundreds of independent data points. The p-value that comes out is not the one your experiment earned.

This is pseudoreplication, and it is the half of the job that quietly breaks papers. The cells in one dish are not independent of each other — they shared a culture, an infection event, an imaging session. They are correlated. Counting each one as a separate observation inflates your sample size roughly two-hundred-fold, shrinks your standard errors by about fourteen, and manufactures significance out of differences that may be biologically trivial. A gap of a hundredth of a square micron can come out "highly significant" purely because the pseudo-N is enormous.

The actual problem

Here is the thing that took me an embarrassingly long time to internalise: the statistical unit in a microscopy experiment is the dish, not the cell. Each independent culture is one realisation of your experimental conditions. The hundreds of cells you measured inside it are repeated measurements of that one realisation — they sharpen your estimate of that dish's size distribution, but they do not add independent evidence about whether your treatment works. It is the same reason weighing yourself ten times on the same scale gives you one weight measured well, not ten weights.

Reviewers at the journals I care about know this. High-impact journals routinely flag and reject microscopy papers with pseudoreplicated analyses. So the failure mode is not just intellectual — it is practical and expensive. You run the experiment, you do the "obvious" analysis, you write the paper, and then someone who has seen this a hundred times sends it back. Or worse, it gets through, and the result does not hold up.

What I wanted was a tool that did the right thing by default — not a tool that let me do the right thing if I remembered to, but one where the correct analysis was the path of least resistance. Summarise each replicate first. Test at the level the design actually supports. Show the reader both the cells and the replicates, honestly, in the same figure. And do all of this without making me hand-roll the plumbing every time I switched to a different measurement variable or a different experiment.

What I built — and what it became

CellMorphR does three things in a fixed order, and the order is the point. It displays every cell — violins, strips, sina plots, ECDFs — so you can see the biological heterogeneity that makes single-cell data worth collecting. It summarises at the replicate level, collapsing those hundreds of cells to one median per dish. And it tests on those summaries, so the p-values reflect the sample size the experiment truly has. Both a two-way ANOVA on replicate medians and, for more power, a linear mixed model over all the cell-level data with replicate as a random effect.

The distribution plots overlay the per-replicate medians on top of the cell cloud, following the SuperPlots idea — so the reader can see, in one glance, both the shape of the data and the small handful of independent points the inference actually rests on. There is a dedicated replicate-only dot plot for when you want to show just those points and nothing else. Pairwise Welch tests, Holm-corrected, tell you which time points diverge. Effect sizes — percent change, Cohen's d — say how big the effect is independent of the p-value, which matters enormously when N is three and power is thin.

It ships two ways because I work two ways. The R Shiny app is for exploring — flipping between measurement variables, trying plot styles, tuning a figure until it is right. The Python command-line tool is for pipelines and batches, and it produces numerically identical results to the R version on the same data, verified to several decimal places. Same ANOVA, same Welch tests, same Holm correction. The choice of tool never changes the science.

More recently I went back and fixed the presentation details that a careful reviewer or copy-editor would otherwise catch. The default palette is now Okabe-Ito, so figures are legible to colourblind readers. Axis titles are unit-aware — set the unit once and it renders as Area (µm²) everywhere. And the replicate count in every caption is now computed from the data rather than hard-coded, so a figure never quietly claims an N the experiment does not have. None of this touched the statistics; the anti-pseudoreplication core was left exactly as it was, because that is the part worth protecting.

On building tools as a scientist

There is a school of thought that says building tools is a distraction from doing science — get the data, analyse it, write it up, move on. There is real wisdom in that. Time at the keyboard is time not at the bench.

But I have come to believe the opposite is often true, especially for a problem like this one. Writing the analysis code forced me to understand pseudoreplication far more deeply than reading about it ever did. When you have to decide, in actual code, what a "replicate" is and at what level a test should run, you cannot hand-wave. You have to confront the fact that replicate "1" in your infected condition is a different dish from replicate "1" in your control, and that if you feed those to a mixed model naively it will silently treat them as the same group and hand you a wrong answer. I only really learned that because I had to write the line that fixes it.

Building the tool forces you to understand the problem at a depth that using tools never does.

CellMorphR is not finished — it has gaps I know about and probably some I do not. It does not yet handle within-replicate cell tracking, or nested imaging-plate designs, or Bayesian hierarchical models. But it is real, it runs, both interfaces agree, and it has already kept my own analyses honest. That feels like the right bar for whether something was worth building. And in this age, when a specific scientific frustration can become a working tool in an afternoon, I think more of us should. You can just build things.

A note on how it was built

CellMorphR was built collaboratively with Claude (Anthropic's AI). The statistical thinking, the design decisions, the insistence on per-replicate analysis, and the frustration that started the whole thing — those are mine. Claude was the implementation partner: writing and debugging the Shiny reactives, the ANOVA and mixed-model plumbing, the plotting code across both languages, and the fiddly work of keeping the R and Python outputs numerically identical. I am good at building tools with AI; I am not a career software engineer, and I would rather be honest about that than pretend otherwise. Working this way let me turn a real statistical problem in my own lab into something other people can use, faster than I could have alone.

Getting started

From spreadsheet to defensible figure

One tidy file in, publication-ready figures and honest statistics out. Pick the R app for exploring or the Python CLI for pipelines — the numbers come out the same.

01

Shape your data

One row per cell, with Condition, Time_min, and Replicate columns plus any numeric measurements (Area, Perimeter, Circularity…). All conditions and time points live in one file.

02

Label replicates honestly

Every image from the same dish gets the same Replicate number. Technical replicates pool automatically — that is what makes your N the count of dishes, not the count of cells.

03

Load it and pick your measure

Drop your file into the Shiny app, or point the CLI at it. Choose the measurement variable and the control condition. No data? Load the built-in demo with a known ground-truth effect.

04

See the distributions and trajectories

Violin, strip, sina, box, ridge, ECDF, or replicate-only dot plot — with per-replicate medians overlaid. Then read the trajectory plot: diverging lines are the interaction you'll report.

05

Run the statistics

Two-way ANOVA on replicate summaries, or a linear mixed model. Read the Condition × Time interaction row, check the Welch pairwise comparisons, and glance at the auto-run assumption diagnostics.

06

Export the figure

Compose a labelled A/B/C panel figure and export as PDF, SVG, EPS, PNG, or TIFF at your target DPI. Save the ANOVA and pairwise results as text and CSV alongside it.
