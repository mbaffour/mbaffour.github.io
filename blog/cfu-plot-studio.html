What it does

The whole CFU-figure loop, in one reproducible app

CFU Plot Studio is built around the exact steps that turn a table of colony counts into a manuscript figure — mapping, plotting, testing, checking, and exporting — without leaving the app.

📥

Flexible CSV import with column mapping

Upload replicate-level counts in whatever layout your lab already uses. Map sample/vector, treatment/dose, timepoint, replicate, and CFU columns inside the app — no fixed column order, no reshaping the spreadsheet by hand.

📊

Publication-ready bar plots

Grouped, log-scaled bar plots with individual replicate points drawn on top. Combined multi-sample views faceted by time, a single sample across both timepoints, or a clean single-panel plot for one timepoint.

📐

Statistics on log10(CFU)

Welch or Student t-tests on log10-transformed counts, or model-based marginal means through emmeans and broom — the right scale for values that span orders of magnitude. Compare samples, timepoints, treatment-vs-control, or every treatment pair.

🔗

Multiple-comparison correction

Adjust p-values with Benjamini-Hochberg, Holm, or Bonferroni — or turn correction off for a single planned contrast. The method you chose is recorded so the figure and its numbers never drift apart.

✳️

Significance annotations

Show comparisons as significance stars or as exact adjusted p/q values placed above the bars — or hide them entirely for a clean version for a talk. Label color, size, and position are all under your control.

📏

Error bars that state their own definition

SD, SEM, 95% CI, IQR, or full range — as capped bars, uncapped whiskers, a mean point with whiskers, or crossbar intervals. Sample size is carried alongside so a reader knows how many replicates each bar summarizes.

📝

Auto methods caption

The figure captions itself: it names the error-bar type and, when statistics are shown, the test and the correction method. The sentence that belongs under the figure is generated for you, and updates as you change settings.

🎨

Okabe-Ito colorblind-safe palette

One click applies the Okabe-Ito qualitative palette (Wong 2011, Nat. Methods) to sample, timepoint, and single-bar colors — a colorblind-safe default so your figure reads correctly for every reviewer. Every other color is still fully editable.

🖨️

Many export formats

PNG at your chosen DPI, vector PDF and SVG for downstream editing, an animated GIF of the bars revealing, and PowerPoint — including editable rvg vector slides you can ungroup and restyle in PowerPoint itself.

🧾

Reproducible R-script export

Download the exact R script that produced the current figure. It is a runnable starting point for recreating the graph outside Shiny — the single most useful thing to keep with a manuscript figure.

✅

Figure QA checklist

A pre-export pass flags low replicate counts, hidden replicate points, low DPI, undersized figures, small fonts, crowded axis labels, clipped statistic labels, and inside-legends that may cover data — before a reviewer does.

🎚️

Full figure geometry control

Exact width, height, and DPI with single-column, double-column, and square presets. Manual y-axis limits, major and minor tick spacing, grid lines, plot box, bar and dodge width, point size, alpha, and jitter — set once, reuse everywhere.

💾

Named style presets

Save the whole visual style — colors, axes, labels, geometry — as a JSON preset and reload it for the next experiment. Your figures stay consistent across a paper without redialing every slider.

🗂️

Data & analysis products, exported too

Cleaned CSV, summary and QC tables, the statistics and ANOVA tables, plus an analysis manifest recording the mapped columns, plot mode, and options. Everything behind the figure is preserved, not just the picture.

🌱

Safe synthetic template data

A neutral, synthetic example dataset ships with the app and can be downloaded as a formatting template — so you can learn the tool, or file a reproducible bug report, without touching unpublished lab data.

Why this exists

The story behind CFU Plot Studio

A colony-forming unit assay is one of the simplest things you do in a microbiology lab. You dilute, you plate, you incubate, you count. The bench part is honest work. The figure part is where it quietly falls apart.

Because a single CFU experiment is never just one number. It is several strains or vectors, a couple of treatments, an early and a late timepoint, three replicate plates each, and counts that live across five or six orders of magnitude. By the time you want to show that data, you are no longer a microbiologist — you are a person copying numbers between a spreadsheet, a stats function, and a plotting tool, hoping you did not transpose a column somewhere along the way.

The counting is the easy part. Turning the counts into a figure I trust, and could reproduce a year later, was the part that kept costing me an afternoon.

So I did what I have started doing whenever I hit a wall like this: I remembered that you can just build things.

The actual problem with CFU figures

To be clear, the individual tools are fine. A spreadsheet is fine for storing counts. R and ggplot2 are excellent for plotting. GraphPad makes lovely graphs. The problem is not any one tool — it is the loop you run to assemble a CFU figure by hand, and how fragile that loop is.

It goes like this. Copy the colony counts out of the plate-reading notes. Compute means and standard deviations. Reshape the table into whatever wide or long shape the plotting tool wants. Decide whether you are on a raw scale or a log scale, and toggle it, and toggle it back. Run a t-test somewhere off to the side. Hand-add significance stars in Illustrator because the plot did not put them where you wanted. Export. Discover the DPI is wrong, or the font is too small, or the legend is sitting on top of a bar. Fix it. Re-export. Then do the whole thing again for the second panel, and again for the next experiment.

The real cost is not the minutes. It is the fragmentation. Your figure exists at once as a spreadsheet, a stats output pasted into a doc, a plotting script, and an Illustrator file with the stars nudged into place — and no single thing that ties them together. When a reviewer asks you to add a correction method or drop a timepoint, you go back to the start of the chain and hope you can remember exactly what you did.

And CFU data has its own specific trap: because the counts are log-distributed, the statistics really ought to run on log10(CFU), not on the raw counts. That is easy to get subtly wrong when the test lives in a different window from the plot. The number under the figure and the bars in the figure should never be able to disagree — but by hand, they can.

What I built

CFU Plot Studio collapses that whole loop into one R/Shiny app. You upload your replicate-level CSV, tell the app which columns mean sample, treatment, timepoint, replicate, and count — no fixed column order, because every lab names these things differently — and it does the rest on the same underlying measurements the whole way through.

It makes the bar plot, with the individual replicate points drawn on top so a reader can see whether a result is carried by consistent replicates or by one lucky plate. It runs the statistics on log10(CFU) — Welch or Student t-tests, or model-based means through emmeans — with your choice of Benjamini-Hochberg, Holm, or Bonferroni correction. It places the significance annotations for you, as stars or as adjusted values, or hides them for a clean version. It draws error bars whose definition — SD, SEM, 95% CI, IQR, or range — is a setting, not a guess, with the sample size carried along.

Then it helps you finish. A Figure QA checklist flags the boring, submission-killing problems — low DPI, fonts too small for print, replicate points switched off, a legend covering data. And when the figure is right, it exports: PNG, vector PDF and SVG, animated GIF, and PowerPoint, including editable rvg slides you can ungroup in PowerPoint itself. Two touches I am fond of: a one-click Okabe-Ito colorblind-safe palette so the figure reads correctly for every reviewer, and an auto methods caption that writes the sentence that belongs under the figure — naming the error-bar type, the test, and the correction.

The piece I care most about is the last one: it exports the exact R script that made the figure, along with a preset and an analysis manifest. So the graph, the numbers, and the recipe stay attached to each other. Future me can reconstruct exactly what was plotted and how — which is the whole point.

On building tools as a scientist

There is a version of scientific training that treats building tools as a distraction from the real work. Get the data, analyze it, write it up, move on. There is genuine wisdom in that — time spent on infrastructure is time not spent at the bench, and a half-built tool can eat more time than it saves.

But there is another version I have come to believe more as my PhD has gone on: building the tool forces you to understand the problem more honestly than using one ever does. Deciding that CFU statistics belong on the log scale, that error bars must announce which interval they are, that the sample size has to travel with the bar, that the methods sentence should be generated rather than typed from memory — every one of those was a small decision about what a trustworthy figure actually is. You do not get to hand-wave those when you are the one writing the defaults.

Building a tool forces you to understand the problem at a depth that using tools never does.

I am not claiming this is finished software. It has gaps I know about and probably some I do not. But it runs, it is real, and it has already saved me more time than it took to build. For a lab utility, that feels like the right bar for whether it was worth doing.

A note on how it was built

CFU Plot Studio was built collaboratively with Claude (Anthropic's AI). The ideas, the workflow, the frustration that motivated it, and the decisions about what a correct CFU figure should do — those are mine. Claude was the implementation partner: writing and debugging the Shiny UI, the ggplot rendering, the log10 statistics and correction logic, and the export code. I am not a career software engineer; I am a biologist who has gotten good at building the tools my own work needs, with an AI partner doing the heavy lifting on implementation. That is worth being transparent about — and it is a large part of why a single frustrated afternoon could turn into a working, shareable app at all.

Getting started

From a CSV to a finished figure

The app follows the shape of a real lab workflow: map, plot, test, check, export. Start with the built-in synthetic dataset if you want to learn it without touching your own data.

01

Upload or load data

Drop in a replicate-level CSV, one row per plate, or load the neutral synthetic template. The data stays at replicate level so the plot and the statistics come from the same measurements.

02

Map your columns

Tell the app which columns mean sample/vector, treatment/dose, timepoint, replicate, and CFU count. Any naming scheme works — Group, Strain, Vector, Construct, whatever your sheet already uses.

03

Choose scope and variation

Pick the plot mode — combined and faceted by time, one sample across time, or a single timepoint — and choose how spread is shown: SD, SEM, 95% CI, IQR, or range, with replicate points on top.

04

Run the statistics

Select the comparison and a test on log10(CFU), then a correction: BH, Holm, Bonferroni, or none. Show results as stars or adjusted values — the methods caption updates to match.

05

Style and run Figure QA

Set axis limits, ticks, colors, and figure size — or apply the Okabe-Ito palette in one click. Then run the Figure QA checklist to catch low DPI, small fonts, and clipped labels before export.

06

Export and archive

Download the figure as PNG, PDF, SVG, GIF, or PowerPoint, and keep the statistics table, the plot preset, and the reproducible R script beside it — so the figure can always be rebuilt.
