What it does

Five tools for the messy first mile of sequence analysis

Genomics Kitchen bundles the small, repetitive prep steps that usually live as scattered one-off scripts — into a single browser suite that keeps your data local and your methods reproducible.

🧬

SeqSieve — exact deduplication with provenance

Collapse exact FASTA/FASTQ duplicates while keeping a representative record for each group. Get counts, a mapping table, duplicate-group listings, and reproducibility metadata — so you can always trace what was kept and why.

🔎

ReadLens — FASTQ QC & quality plots

Inspect sequence length, base composition, GC content, and FASTQ Phred+33 mean-quality distributions. Duplicate signals, invalid characters, and parser warnings are surfaced instead of swallowed.

🧩

SeqCompare — core / accessory / pairwise

Compare two or more FASTA files by exact sequence, ID, or full record. Export core, accessory, file-specific, shared-by-subset, presence/absence, and pairwise-overlap results with Jaccard similarity.

🔦

ORFScout — six-frame ORF finding

Six-frame translation and candidate ORF discovery, with coordinates reported as 1-based inclusive positions. A colorblind-safe genome track shows forward and reverse strands with a clear legend.

⚒️

HMMForge — protein-family cleanup & recipes

Clean protein FASTA files, generate safe alignment IDs, flag outliers, and preserve duplicate mappings. Then get ready-to-paste MAFFT and HMMER command recipes for the alignment and profile steps.

🔒

Local-only, by design

No backend, no upload, no tracking, no analytics, and no hidden network calls. Your sequences are parsed in the browser and stay on your machine — which is what makes it safe for unpublished or sensitive data.

🎨

Colorblind-safe charts

Every figure uses the Okabe–Ito qualitative palette and a single viridis mid-tone for distributions — no false second dimension encoded in hue, and readable for the common forms of color-vision deficiency.

🖼️

SVG & PNG figure export

QC histograms are inline SVG with axis titles, units, and numeric ticks. Export them as vector SVG or as PNG at ≥2× device pixel ratio — crisp enough to drop straight into a slide or a manuscript figure.

📊

Per-table CSV export

Every quantitative panel — QC metrics, duplicate groups, predicted ORFs, pairwise comparisons, protein classifications — carries a one-click Download CSV that writes the full row set, not just the on-screen preview.

📝

Methods-ready outputs

Each tool produces methods language you can paste into a manuscript, a summary TXT, and a reproducibility JSON recording parameters and versions. Filenames follow a dated, tool-tagged convention automatically.

🧾

Honest about the biology

Exact matching is labeled as exact matching. ORF prediction is never dressed up as annotation. HMMForge prepares inputs and recipes rather than pretending the browser can replace MAFFT or HMMER.

🧪

Built-in validation page

Run parser, reverse-complement, translation, ORF-coordinate, deduplication, QC, comparison, HMMForge, and export checks from a single page — so you can confirm the tool behaves as documented before you trust its output.

⚠️

Honest memory warnings

Large files are bounded by browser memory, so Genomics Kitchen warns above 50 MB and strongly warns above 250 MB. Big tables are previewed in the UI and exported in full — no silent truncation.

Why this exists

The story behind Genomics Kitchen

Bioinformatics almost never begins with a clean, obedient file. It begins with a FASTA someone renamed three times, a FASTQ with suspicious quality tails, a handful of duplicate records that may or may not matter, and a folder of sequence sets that need to be compared before anyone can make a confident next move. That first mile — the prep before the analysis — is where I kept losing time.

I am a phage biologist. Most of my days are about isolating phages, assembling genomes, and trying to make sense of what is actually encoded in them. And every one of those days seemed to include the same small chores: dedup this file, check the quality on that one, find the open reading frames, compare these two isolates, clean up a protein family before it goes into an alignment. None of it is hard. All of it is fiddly. And all of it lived as a pile of one-off scripts and half-remembered command-line incantations scattered across my machine and my lab's shared drive.

The tools existed. They just did not live in one place, and half of them only ran on the one machine where I had gotten the environment working.

So I did what I have started doing whenever I hit that kind of wall: I remembered that, in this age of AI, you can just build things.

The actual problem with sequence prep

To be clear, the heavy machinery of bioinformatics is excellent. CD-HIT, MMseqs2, VSEARCH, MAFFT, HMMER — these are the tools I reach for when the job is real and large, and nothing I build is trying to replace them. Genomics Kitchen is emphatically not a substitute for CD-HIT when you need identity-threshold clustering, nor for MAFFT or HMMER when you are building a real profile. It is the fast, reproducible prep layer that sits in front of all of that.

The problem was never that good tools do not exist. The problem was fragmentation. To dedup a file, check its quality, and scan it for ORFs, I was moving between three different scripts with three different invocation styles, three different output formats, and three different sets of assumptions about what my input looked like. Each one needed an install, an environment, a remembered flag. A labmate who wanted to do the same quick check either had to reproduce my whole setup or ask me to run it for them.

And there was a quieter cost underneath all of it: the data. A lot of what I work with is unpublished. Uploading a sequence set to some random web tool to get a quick quality plot is exactly the kind of thing you are not supposed to do, and yet the convenient tools are so often the ones that phone home. I wanted the convenience without the exposure.

What I built — and what it became

Genomics Kitchen is a browser-based suite of five tools for that first mile. You open a page. You drop in your files. Everything is parsed and processed locally in your browser — no backend, no upload, no analytics, no hidden network calls. When you are done, you export methods-ready text, reproducibility JSON, and spreadsheet-ready CSVs, and you close the tab. Nothing was ever anywhere but your own machine.

The five tools map onto the chores I kept repeating. SeqSieve collapses exact duplicates while keeping full provenance — counts, a mapping table, duplicate groups — so a dedup step is never a black box. ReadLens gives me length, composition, GC, and FASTQ quality distributions with the warnings surfaced instead of swallowed. SeqCompare takes a folder of FASTA files and tells me what is core, accessory, or file-specific, with pairwise overlap and Jaccard similarity. ORFScout does six-frame translation and candidate ORF discovery with honest, 1-based inclusive coordinates. And HMMForge cleans up a protein family, assigns safe IDs, and hands me the exact MAFFT and HMMER commands to run next.

The part I care most about is the honesty of the labels. Exact matching is called exact matching. ORF prediction is never sold as annotation. HMMForge prepares inputs and recipes rather than pretending a browser can run HMMER. A big part of building a scientific tool is refusing to overstate what it does — and I wanted that discipline baked into every panel.

The most recent round of work pushed the output toward publication quality: colorblind-safe Okabe–Ito palettes, axis titles with real units, SVG and high-DPI PNG figure export, and a one-click CSV on every quantitative table. If a number is on the screen, you can get it out cleanly, and any figure you export should be legible to a colorblind reviewer and crisp enough for a manuscript.

On building tools as a scientist

There is a version of scientific training that treats tool-building as a distraction from the real work. Get the data, analyze it, write it up, move on. And there is genuine wisdom in that — time spent building infrastructure is time not spent at the bench.

But I have come to believe, more and more as my PhD has gone on, that building a tool forces you to understand a problem at a depth that merely using tools never does. Writing SeqCompare made me define, precisely, what I mean by "core" versus "accessory" — and confront the fact that shared exact sequence identity is not the same thing as evolutionary homology. Writing ORFScout made me be exact about coordinate conventions. You cannot hand-wave when the code has to actually produce the number.

Building a tool forces you to understand a problem at a depth that merely using tools never does.

I am not a career software engineer, and I am not pretending to be one. What I am is a scientist who has gotten good at building the tools I need, with AI as a partner. Genomics Kitchen is not finished — it has gaps I know about and probably gaps I do not. But it runs, it is honest about what it does, and it has already made my own prep less painful. That feels like the right bar for whether something was worth building.

A note on how it was built

Genomics Kitchen was built collaboratively with Claude (Anthropic's AI). The ideas, the scientific framing, the frustration that motivated it, and the decisions about what to build and what to refuse to build — those are mine. Claude was the implementation partner: writing and debugging the React components, the parsers, the deduplication and comparison logic, the figure export, and the validation checks. Working this way let me turn a specific, recurring lab frustration into a working suite far faster than I could have alone, and I think it is worth being transparent about that.

Getting started

Open it and start prepping

There is nothing to install. Open the app, pick a tool, drop in your files, and export what you need. Everything happens in your browser — your sequences never leave the tab.

01

Open in your browser

Go to the live app — no download, no account, no sign-up. It runs entirely client-side, so your data stays on your machine from the first click.

02

Pick a tool

SeqSieve for dedup, ReadLens for QC, SeqCompare for multi-file comparison, ORFScout for ORFs, HMMForge for protein-family prep. Each has its own focused panel.

03

Drop in your files

Load FASTA, multi-FASTA, or FASTQ four-line records — nucleotide or protein. Genomics Kitchen warns above 50 MB and strongly warns above 250 MB, since files are bounded by browser memory.

04

Review the results

Read the counts, tables, and colorblind-safe plots. Duplicate groups, parser warnings, ORF coordinates, and comparison keys are all shown — nothing important is hidden.

05

Export methods-ready output

Grab per-table CSVs, TSV tables, reproducibility JSON, SVG/PNG figures, and methods language for your manuscript. Filenames follow genomics-kitchen_TOOL_INPUT_YYYYMMDD_HHMM.ext.

06

Verify with the Validation page

Run the built-in parser, translation, ORF-coordinate, dedup, QC, comparison, and export checks to confirm the tool behaves as documented before you rely on it.
