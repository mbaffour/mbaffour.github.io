Why I built this

Counting bacteriophage plaques is one of those tasks every phage lab does constantly and nobody enjoys. You squint at a plate, tally clearings by eye, guess at which ones are “turbid,” and hope the person next to you would have counted the same way. It is slow, it is subjective, and it quietly injects noise into every titer you report.

I wanted the measurement to come from the image, not from my mood at 6 pm. So I built a toolkit that takes a photo of a plate and returns real numbers: how many plaques, how big, how turbid, and the resulting titer - the same way every time, whether it runs on my laptop or in a script.

The goal was simple: make plaque scoring objective and reproducible, so two people (or the same person on two days) get the same answer from the same plate.

What it does

📷

Photo to measurements

Drop in a Petri-dish image and get per-plaque size, turbidity, count, and calculated titer.

🔬

Published + in-house engines

Wraps the peer-reviewed Plaque Size Tool alongside in-house detection models for sensitive or precise modes.

🖥️

Desktop app or CLI

A friendly PySide6/Qt interface for the bench, plus a command-line mode for batch and scripted runs.

♻️

Reproducible by default

Same image in, same numbers out - no eyeballing, no drift between people or days.

How it works

  1. Photograph the plate

    Any reasonably lit photo of the plate works - phone photos included.

  2. Pick an engine

    Choose the published method or an in-house model depending on how sensitive or precise you need to be.

  3. Read the numbers

    Get counts, sizes, turbidity, and titer - ready to drop into your notebook or analysis.

Why it matters

Plaque assays sit under a huge amount of phage work - titering stocks, comparing host ranges, screening mutants. Making that first measurement objective means everything built on top of it is a little more trustworthy. This is my newest and most involved tool, and the one I lean on most in my own research.
