What it does

The plaque count, made objective

Plaque Toolkit takes the task every phage lab does constantly — counting and sizing clearings on a plate — and moves it off the eyeball and onto the pixel.

🔬

Per-plaque size in mm and px

Area (px² and mm²) and diameter (px and mm) for every plaque on the plate. Pass your dish diameter with -p 100 and the pixel math is calibrated to real millimeters; without it, values stay in pixels.

🌫️

Turbidity, measured not guessed

Instead of eyeballing "clear vs. turbid," it reports grayscale intensity (MEAN_GRAY, 0–255) and a within-plate relative clarity (TURBIDITY_REL) for each plaque — a number you can sort, filter, and defend.

🔢

Counts and titer

Total plaques per plate, and PFU/mL calculated straight from the counts. The same plate photographed twice gives the same count — the noise you were quietly adding to every titer is gone.

📖

A published engine you can cite

The Published mode is the peer-reviewed Plaque Size Tool (Trofimova & Jaschke, Virology 2021) — independently validated, and suitable for a paper without extra validation work on your end.

🎯

Precise mode for dense plates

Best detection for crowded, countable plates: an in-house PlaqSeg YOLO segmentation model, the Plaque Size Tool, and a learned plaque-vs-texture classifier working together to pull plaques apart.

🔍

Sensitive mode for tiny plaques

Lowered size gates catch sub-0.4 mm plaques that a default pass skips — useful for exploratory work where the small clearings are exactly the ones you care about.

✏️

Interactive editor

Detection is a starting point, not the final word. Open the GUI editor and click to add a plaque the model missed or remove a speck it shouldn't have counted. The canvas stays editable until you're happy.

📁

Batch a whole folder

Drop a folder of plate photos and get one CSV per plate, automatically. The overnight scan you took can be measured before your coffee's done.

📊

Cross-phage comparison

The Compare Turbidity tool measures absolute optical density across phages and assembles publication-ready box plots and histograms for multi-phage comparisons.

📐

Scale-bar stamping

Overlay a physical "5 mm" reference directly onto the annotated photo, so the figure that lands in your slide deck or manuscript carries its own scale.

📱

Reads iPhone HEIC photos

The plate photo on your phone works as-is. Native HEIC conversion means you don't have to round-trip every image through a converter before measuring.

💾

One-click export

The green "Export all" button writes the CSV of measurements and the annotated figure together, in one step. Plaques are numbered row by row from the top so numbers on the image match rows in the table.

🖥️

GUI or command line, your call

A PySide6/Qt desktop app for point-and-click work, and a batch CLI for pipelines and headless runs. Eight drag-and-drop .bat launchers cover the common jobs without opening a terminal.

Why this exists

The story behind Plaque Toolkit

In this post

The predicament The actual problem What I built On building tools Built with Claude

Counting plaques is one of those tasks every phage lab does constantly and nobody enjoys. You take a plate off the bench, hold it up to the light, and start tallying clearings by eye. You squint at the borderline ones — is that a real plaque or a bubble in the agar? You decide, mostly on feel, which ones look "turbid" and which look "clear." Then you write a number down, dilute it out to a titer, and move on. It is the most routine measurement in phage biology, and it is almost entirely subjective.

The part that finally got to me was not the tedium. It was the realization of how much the number depended on me — on which of us was counting, on whether it was the first plate of the day or the last, on whether I was fresh or it was 6 pm and I wanted to go home. Two people counting the same plate can land on different numbers, and both feel confident. That difference does not announce itself. It just quietly rides along into the titer, into the figure, into the paper.

So I did what I have started doing whenever I hit a wall like this: I remembered that, in this age of AI, you can just build things.

The actual problem

The problem with counting plaques by eye is not that people are careless. Phage biologists are careful — that is the whole job. The problem is that the human visual system is not a measuring instrument. It is fast and adaptive and context-dependent, which is exactly what you do not want when the thing you are measuring is a faint clearing whose edge fades gradually into confluent lysis.

Two failures compound. First, the count itself: dense plates are genuinely hard, and where one person sees three overlapping plaques another sees one big one. Second, and worse, is turbidity. "Turbid" and "clear" are a spectrum, not a binary, and everyone draws the line in a slightly different place. When that judgment feeds a phenotype — this mutant makes clearer plaques than wild type — you are building a claim on top of a measurement nobody can reproduce, not even you a week later.

The measurement should come from the image, not from my mood at 6 pm. The same plate should give the same number every time.

That is the entire design goal. Not to replace the scientist's judgment, but to move the part that should be objective — where is the plaque, how big is it, how much light passes through it — off the eyeball and onto the pixel, so that judgment can be spent where it actually belongs.

What I built

Plaque Toolkit takes a photo of a Petri dish and returns, for every plaque on the plate, its size in pixels and millimeters, its grayscale intensity, a relative turbidity, and — for the plate as a whole — a count and a titer in PFU/mL. It runs as a PySide6/Qt desktop app for point-and-click work, and as a command-line tool for batch runs across a whole folder of plates. It reads iPhone HEIC photos directly, stamps a physical scale bar onto the annotated figure, and writes everything out as a CSV plus an image in one click.

The important decision was not to trust any single detector. Plaque Toolkit ships four engines. The Published engine is the peer-reviewed Plaque Size Tool from Trofimova and Jaschke — independently validated, the one to cite when a result goes into a paper. Around it I built three in-house modes: a Current default with bug fixes and calibration corrections, a Sensitive mode that lowers the size gates to catch sub-0.4 mm plaques, and a Precise mode that combines a PlaqSeg YOLO segmentation model, the Plaque Size Tool, and a learned plaque-vs-texture classifier for dense, crowded plates.

I want to be honest about a limit here, because the tool is honest about it too: only the Published engine is independently validated. The in-house modes are useful but unvalidated, and the repository ships a whole document on how to validate them on your own plates before you publish from them. A tool that quietly overstates its own reliability is worse than no tool. So this one tells you exactly how far to trust each mode.

Detection is also never the last word. Every result opens in an editor where you click to add a plaque the model missed or remove a speck it shouldn't have counted. The goal was never to take the scientist out of the loop — it was to give them a defensible starting point instead of a blank tally sheet.

On building tools as a scientist

There is a school of thought that says a biologist building software is a biologist not doing biology. There is truth in it — every hour on tooling is an hour off the bench. But I have come to believe the opposite is often true: building the tool is how you finally understand the measurement.

When I had to write the turbidity code, I could not hide behind "clear" and "turbid" anymore. I had to decide what turbidity actually is in terms of pixels — mean gray value relative to the surrounding lawn — and defend that choice. Writing the calibration meant confronting exactly how a dish diameter in millimeters maps to pixels, and what breaks when someone forgets to pass it. You cannot fake your way through that. The code forces a precision of thought that eyeballing a plate never demands.

This is the newest tool I have built and the one I now reach for most. I am not going to pretend it is finished or flawless — it has gaps I know about and probably some I don't. But it runs, it gives the same answer twice, and it has already changed how I trust my own plate counts. That feels like the right bar for whether something was worth building.

A note on how it was built

Plaque Toolkit was built collaboratively with Claude (Anthropic's AI). The scientific decisions are mine — what to measure, why turbidity is defined the way it is, which engine should be the citable one, and the frustration of counting by eye that started the whole thing. Claude was the implementation partner: the Qt interface, the YOLO and classifier plumbing, the CSV and figure export, the calibration math. I am good at building tools with AI; I am not a career software engineer, and I would rather say that plainly than pretend otherwise. Working this way let me turn a real lab annoyance into a working instrument far faster than I could have alone, and I think that transparency is worth stating out loud.

Getting started

From plate photo to CSV in a few steps

Install once, then either double-click a launcher or run the command line. See INSTALL.md and USER_GUIDE.md in the repo for platform details.

01

Install it

Run the desktop installer on Windows, or build from source on any OS with conda env create -f environment.yml. macOS on Apple Silicon is supported.

02

Drop in a plate photo

Drag a photo or a whole folder onto Measure Plaques.bat, or open the desktop app and load an image. iPhone HEIC files work directly — no conversion step.

03

Pick an engine

Current for routine work, Sensitive for tiny plaques, Precise for dense plates, or Published when the result is going into a paper. Pass your dish diameter (e.g. -p 100) to get millimeters.

04

Edit the detections

Open the interactive editor and click to add any plaque the model missed or remove a speck it shouldn't have counted. Plaques are numbered row by row from the top.

05

Export everything

Hit the green "Export all" button. Plaque Toolkit writes a CSV of per-plaque size, gray value, and turbidity plus an annotated figure with a stamped scale bar.

06

Compare across phages

Run Compare Turbidity for absolute optical density across phages, then let it assemble box plots and histograms for a multi-phage figure — and a titer in PFU/mL.
